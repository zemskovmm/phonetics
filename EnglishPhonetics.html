Complete English Accent and IPA Guide

This guide focuses mainly on General American English, but important British/RP differences are also noted.

IPA notation:

  • / / = phoneme, the sound category in the language.
  • [ ] = exact pronunciation or allophone.

Example: English /t/ can be pronounced as [tʰ], [ɾ], or [ʔ] depending on position and accent.

1. English Consonants in IPA

Stops / Plosives

IPA Example Articulation
/p/ pie /paɪ/ Voiceless bilabial stop. Close both lips, build air pressure, then release. In stressed word-initial position, it is strongly aspirated: pie [pʰaɪ].
/b/ buy /baɪ/ Voiced bilabial stop. Same lip closure as /p/, but vocal folds vibrate.
/t/ tie /taɪ/ Voiceless alveolar stop. Tongue tip or blade touches the alveolar ridge behind the upper teeth. Often aspirated word-initially: [tʰ]. In American English, between vowels it often becomes a flap [ɾ], as in water.
/d/ die /daɪ/ Voiced alveolar stop. Same tongue position as /t/, but voiced.
/k/ cat /kæt/ Voiceless velar stop. Back of tongue contacts the soft palate. Aspirated initially: [kʰ].
/g/ go /goʊ/ Voiced velar stop. Same tongue position as /k/, with voicing.

Aspiration: /p t k/ are usually aspirated at the beginning of stressed syllables.

  • pin [pʰɪn]
  • top [tʰɑp]
  • cat [kʰæt]

But they are usually not aspirated after /s/:

  • spin [spɪn]
  • stop [stɑp]
  • sky [skaɪ]

Fricatives

IPA Example Articulation
/f/ fan /fæn/ Voiceless labiodental fricative. Upper teeth touch lower lip; blow air.
/v/ van /væn/ Voiced labiodental fricative. Same as /f/, but with vocal fold vibration.
/θ/ thin /θɪn/ Voiceless dental fricative. Tongue tip lightly touches or comes between the teeth; blow air. Do not replace it with /s/ or /t/.
/ð/ this /ðɪs/ Voiced dental fricative. Same tongue position as /θ/, but voiced. Common in function words: the, this, that, they.
/s/ see /si/ Voiceless alveolar fricative. Tongue near alveolar ridge with a narrow groove; sharp air stream.
/z/ zoo /zu/ Voiced alveolar fricative. Same as /s/, with voicing.
/ʃ/ she /ʃi/ Voiceless postalveolar fricative. Tongue blade slightly behind the alveolar ridge; lips often slightly rounded.
/ʒ/ vision /ˈvɪʒən/ Voiced postalveolar fricative. Same as /ʃ/, with voicing. Found in words like vision, measure, genre.
/h/ hat /hæt/ Voiceless glottal fricative. Air passes through open vocal folds. Shape your mouth for the following vowel.

Affricates

Affricates start as stops and release into fricatives.

IPA Example Articulation
/tʃ/ church /tʃɝtʃ/ Voiceless postalveolar affricate. Start like /t/, then release into /ʃ/.
/dʒ/ judge /dʒʌdʒ/ Voiced postalveolar affricate. Start like /d/, then release into /ʒ/.

Nasals

IPA Example Articulation
/m/ man /mæn/ Bilabial nasal. Lips closed; air flows through the nose.
/n/ no /noʊ/ Alveolar nasal. Tongue touches alveolar ridge; air flows through the nose.
/ŋ/ sing /sɪŋ/ Velar nasal. Back of tongue touches the soft palate; air flows through the nose. Do not add /g/ at the end of sing.

Compare:

  • singer /ˈsɪŋɚ/ = no /g/
  • finger /ˈfɪŋgɚ/ = has /g/

Approximants, Liquids, and Glides

IPA Example Articulation
/l/ light /laɪt/ Alveolar lateral approximant. Tongue tip touches the alveolar ridge; air flows around the sides. English has “light L” before vowels and “dark L” [ɫ] after vowels, as in feel [fiɫ].
/ɹ/ red /ɹɛd/ English /r/ is usually an approximant, not a trill. The tongue may be bunched or curled back. Do not roll or tap it in standard American English.
/j/ yes /jɛs/ Palatal glide. Like a quick /i/ sound before a vowel.
/w/ we /wi/ Labial-velar glide. Round the lips and raise the back of the tongue. Do not use teeth/lip friction like /v/.

Optional or accent-specific sounds:

  • /ʍ/ in some accents: which /ʍɪtʃ/ vs witch /wɪtʃ/
  • /x/ in Scottish English or borrowings: loch /lɒx/
  • /ʔ/ glottal stop: common as an allophone of /t/ in some accents, as in button [ˈbʌʔn̩]

2. English Vowels in IPA

English vowels vary significantly by accent. The following table gives a mainstream General American system.

Short/Lax and Long/Tense Vowels

IPA Example Articulation
/i/ fleece /flis/ High front unrounded tense vowel. Smile slightly; tongue high and forward.
/ɪ/ kit /kɪt/ Near-high front/central lax vowel. Tongue lower and more relaxed than /i/.
/ɛ/ dress /drɛs/ Mid-front unrounded vowel. Jaw slightly open.
/æ/ trap /træp/ Low front unrounded vowel. Jaw open, tongue front and low. In American English it is often raised before nasals, so man may sound like [meən].
/ɑ/ lot /lɑt/, father /ˈfɑðɚ/ Low back or central unrounded vowel. Jaw open, tongue low/back, lips neutral.
/ɔ/ thought /θɔt/ Mid-low back rounded vowel. Lips rounded. Many Americans merge /ɔ/ with /ɑ/, so cot and caught may sound the same.
/ʊ/ foot /fʊt/ Near-high back lax rounded vowel. Shorter and more relaxed than /u/.
/u/ goose /gus/ High back rounded tense vowel. In English it is often slightly fronted; lips rounded.
/ʌ/ strut /strʌt/ Mid central/back unrounded vowel. Relaxed and short. Do not pronounce it like /ɑ/.
/ə/ comma /ˈkɑmə/, about /əˈbaʊt/ Schwa. Mid-central unstressed vowel. Extremely common in weak syllables.
/ɝ/ nurse /nɝs/ Stressed r-colored vowel. Tongue is in an /r/ position during the vowel.
/ɚ/ letter /ˈlɛɾɚ/ Unstressed r-colored vowel. Common in American English final -er, -or, -ar.

Diphthongs

Diphthongs move from one vowel position to another.

IPA Example Articulation
/eɪ/ face /feɪs/ Start mid-front and glide toward /ɪ/. Do not make it completely flat.
/oʊ/ goat /goʊt/ Start mid-back rounded and glide toward /ʊ/.
/aɪ/ price /praɪs/ Start low central/back and glide toward /ɪ/.
/aʊ/ mouth /maʊθ/ Start low central/back and glide toward /ʊ/.
/ɔɪ/ choice /tʃɔɪs/ Start rounded /ɔ/ and glide toward /ɪ/.

3. Key Allophones for a Natural English Accent

Aspiration: /p t k/

English aspirates voiceless stops at the beginning of stressed syllables. Practice with a tissue or paper in front of your mouth.

  • pie, tie, kite → paper should move.
  • spy, sty, sky → paper should move much less.

Examples:

  • pie [pʰaɪ]
  • tie [tʰaɪ]
  • kite [kʰaɪt]

American Flap [ɾ]

In American English, /t/ and /d/ often become [ɾ] between vowels when the second vowel is unstressed.

Word Careful IPA Common American Pronunciation
water /ˈwɔtɚ/ or /ˈwɑtɚ/ [ˈwɔɾɚ] or [ˈwɑɾɚ]
better /ˈbɛtɚ/ [ˈbɛɾɚ]
city /ˈsɪti/ [ˈsɪɾi]
ladder /ˈlædɚ/ [ˈlæɾɚ]

The flap is like a very quick tap of the tongue, similar to a single Spanish, Japanese, or Russian tap “r,” but very light.

Dark L [ɫ]

English /l/ is often “dark” after vowels.

  • light [laɪt] = light L
  • feel [fiɫ] = dark L
  • milk [mɪɫk]

For dark L, the tongue tip touches the alveolar ridge, but the back of the tongue also rises.

Final Consonants

Many learners add an extra vowel after final consonants. Avoid this.

  • book = /bʊk/, not “book-uh”
  • bad = /bæd/, not “bad-uh”
  • think = /θɪŋk/, not “think-uh”

Practice ending cleanly:

cap, cab, back, bag, bus, buzz, leaf, leave, teeth, teethe

5. Targeted Articulation Exercises

Exercise 1: /θ/ and /ð/ “TH” Drill

Put your tongue tip lightly between your teeth or just behind your top teeth. Blow air continuously.

Voiceless /θ/:

thin, think, three, bath, teeth, author

Voiced /ð/:

this, that, these, those, mother, breathe

Sentences:

  1. Three thin thieves thought thoughtfully.
  2. This is the thing that they wanted.
  3. My brother and mother are there with them.

Exercise 2: /r/ vs /l/

For /l/, the tongue tip touches the alveolar ridge. For /ɹ/, the tongue does not touch the ridge; the tongue bunches or curls, and the lips may round slightly.

Words:

light/right, long/wrong, led/red, glass/grass, play/pray, climb/crime

Sentences:

  1. Red roses rarely grow near large lakes.
  2. Larry really likes reading long letters.
  3. Please bring the green glass.

Exercise 3: /w/ vs /v/

For /w/, round both lips. No teeth contact. For /v/, upper teeth touch the lower lip.

Words:

wine/vine, west/vest, while/vile, wet/vet, worse/verse

Sentence:

Victor went west with very warm winter clothes.

Exercise 4: /ɪ/ vs /i/

This is one of the most important English vowel contrasts.

  • /i/ = high, tense, longer
  • /ɪ/ = lower, relaxed, shorter

Practice:

  • beat /bit/ vs bit /bɪt/
  • seat /sit/ vs sit /sɪt/
  • leave /liv/ vs live /lɪv/
  • sheep /ʃip/ vs ship /ʃɪp/

Sentence:

The sheep will sit in the green field.

Exercise 5: /æ/ vs /ɛ/

For /æ/, open the jaw more and keep the tongue low/front.

Practice:

  • bad /bæd/ vs bed /bɛd/
  • man /mæn/ vs men /mɛn/
  • sat /sæt/ vs set /sɛt/
  • bag /bæg/ vs beg /bɛg/

Sentence:

The man sat on the bad red bed.

Exercise 6: /ʊ/ vs /u/

For /u/, lips are more rounded and the vowel is longer/tenser. For /ʊ/, the vowel is shorter and more relaxed.

Practice:

  • full /fʊl/ vs fool /ful/
  • pull /pʊl/ vs pool /pul/
  • could /kʊd/ vs cooed /kud/
  • look /lʊk/ vs Luke /luk/

Sentence:

Luke looked at the full pool.

6. Diphthong Practice

English diphthongs need movement. Do not freeze them as one flat vowel.

  • /eɪ/: say, day, face, late
  • /oʊ/: go, no, boat, home
  • /aɪ/: my, time, price, light
  • /aʊ/: now, house, mouth, loud
  • /ɔɪ/: boy, choice, voice, noise

Drill:

  1. Say the first part slowly.
  2. Glide to the second part.
  3. Shorten it until it sounds natural.

Example:

my = /maɪ/ → “maa-ih” → /maɪ/

Sentence:

I know they made a choice about my house.

7. Stress, Rhythm, and Reduction

Accent is not only individual sounds. English is stress-timed: important words are stronger, and function words often reduce.

Common Reductions

Full Word Reduced Form
to /tə/
for /fɚ/ or /fər/
and /ən/, /n/
of /əv/
can /kən/
the /ðə/ before consonants, /ði/ before vowels
a /ə/

Example sentence:

I can go to the store for a cup of coffee.

Careful:

/aɪ kæn goʊ tu ðə stɔr fɔr ə kʌp ʌv kɔfi/

Natural American:

/aɪ kən ˈgoʊ tə ðə ˈstɔr fər ə ˈkʌp əv ˈkɔfi/

The stressed words are:

I can GO to the STORE for a CUP of COFFEE.

8. Daily Practice Routine

Use this 25–30 minute routine.

1. Warm-up: 3 minutes

Say this vowel ladder slowly:

/i ɪ ɛ æ ɑ ɔ ʊ u ʌ ə ɝ/

Example words:

see, sit, set, sat, hot, thought, foot, food, cup, about, bird

2. One Sound Focus: 7 minutes

Pick one difficult sound, for example /θ/.

  1. Isolated sound: /θ θ θ/
  2. Syllables: /θi θeɪ θæ θɑ θu/
  3. Words: thin, think, three, bath
  4. Sentences: Three thin thieves thought thoughtfully.

3. Minimal Pairs: 7 minutes

Choose one contrast:

  • /i/ vs /ɪ/: sheep/ship
  • /æ/ vs /ɛ/: bad/bed
  • /θ/ vs /s/: thin/sin
  • /l/ vs /ɹ/: light/right

Record yourself and listen.

4. Shadowing: 10 minutes

Take 20–30 seconds of native audio.

  1. Listen once without speaking.
  2. Mark stressed words.
  3. Listen and repeat phrase by phrase.
  4. Speak with the audio at the same time.
  5. Record yourself and compare.

5. Free Speech: 3 minutes

Talk about your day while focusing on one target sound. Do not try to fix everything at once.

9. Best Practice Strategy

For each sound, move through this order:

  1. Hear it: Can you identify it?
  2. Produce it alone: Can you make the sound correctly?
  3. Use it in syllables: /pa pi pu/, /θa θi θu/
  4. Use it in words: thin, think, bath
  5. Use it in sentences.
  6. Use it in spontaneous speech.

Do not practice only isolated sounds. The goal is automatic use in real speech.
